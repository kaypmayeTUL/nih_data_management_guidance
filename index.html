What Is the NIH DMS Policy?

A major expansion of data sharing requirements effective January 25, 2023
The NIH Policy for Data Management and Sharing (DMS Policy) requires all NIH-funded or conducted research that generates scientific data to include a formal plan for how that data will be managed and shared. The plan must be submitted as part of the grant application and becomes a binding term and condition of any award.
Jan 25, 2023
Policy effective date — applies to all competing applications submitted on or after this date
All Funding Levels
Previous 2003 policy only required plans for grants with $500,000+ in direct costs per year
⚡ 2026 Update: NIH issued NOT-OD-26-046 on February 25, 2026, updating the required format of the DMSP. The updated format is required for applications with due dates on or after May 25, 2026. The underlying 2023 policy remains unchanged — only the plan format is updated.

Who Must Comply?

The DMS Policy applies broadly — but not universally. Check your activity code.

✅ Covered mechanisms

Research Grants

All R-series grants (R01, R21, R03, R15, R34, etc.) and competing renewals that generate scientific data

Cooperative Agreements

All U-mechanism grants that result in the generation of scientific data

Career Development Awards

Some K-series awards — check the specific FOA for your mechanism

SBIR / STTR Grants

Small business innovation grants — with some exceptions for proprietary data (may withhold up to 20 years)

❌ Not covered

Training (T) Grants

Fellowship and training mechanisms — data generation is not the primary purpose

Fellowships (F)

Individual fellowship awards are not covered under the DMS Policy

Construction (C06) & Infrastructure (S06)

Infrastructure and construction grants that don't generate scientific data

Conference Grants (R13)

Conference and resource grants not generating scientific data

💡 When in doubt: Check the NIH Grants Policy Statement §8.2.3.1 or the specific Funding Opportunity Announcement (FOA) — some ICOs have additional requirements.

Defining "Scientific Data"

The policy applies only to scientific data — not everything your lab produces
Official NIH Definition: "The recorded factual material commonly accepted in the scientific community as of sufficient quality to validate and replicate research findings, regardless of whether the data are used to support scholarly publications."

✅ IS Scientific Data

  • Processed datasets used to support published conclusions
  • Survey responses (when de-identified)
  • Genomic sequences, imaging data, clinical measurements
  • Code or software needed to reproduce research findings
  • Metadata accompanying shared data files
  • Transcripts or qualitative data files (appropriately de-identified)

❌ NOT Scientific Data

  • Laboratory notebooks and preliminary analyses
  • Completed case report forms
  • Drafts of scientific papers and grant applications
  • Plans for future research
  • Peer reviews and colleague communications
  • Physical objects (specimens, slides, tissue)
💡 Practical test: If you wouldn't share it to allow another researcher to replicate your findings, it probably doesn't qualify as scientific data under this policy.

Original DMSP Elements (NOT-OD-21-014)

The six narrative elements required under the 2023 policy — 2 pages or fewer. These remain the standard until May 25, 2026.
1

Data Type

Describe the types, formats, and estimated amount of data to be generated. Include preservation and access needs for each data type.

2

Related Tools, Software & Code

Note any tools, software, or code needed to access or manipulate shared data. Describe how these will be shared if necessary.

3

Standards

Identify metadata standards, data dictionaries, and identifiers to be applied. If no standard exists for your field, state that clearly.

4

Data Preservation, Access & Timeline

Specify where data will be deposited, when it will be made available, and how long it will be preserved. Describe access procedures.

5

Access, Distribution & Reuse

Describe any protections, licenses (e.g., CC0, CC-BY), or access controls applied to shared data. For human subjects data, address consent.

6

Oversight of DMS

Name who at your institution (by title or role) will monitor compliance with the plan, how frequently, and by what methods.

📝 Format: Submitted as a single PDF in the "Other Plan(s)" field of NIH FORMS-H. The plan is assessed by NIH Program Staff — not scored in peer review (unless specifically noted in the FOA).

2026 Format Update: NOT-OD-26-046

Required for applications with due dates on or after May 25, 2026 — shifts from narrative elements to structured Yes/No questions
⚡ What changed: After reviewing over 1,100 DMSP submissions, NIH found many plans included unnecessary detail and exceeded the 2-page limit. The new format uses structured Yes/No questions with explanations required only when the answer is "No" or when sharing will be limited.
Q1
Will there be maximum appropriate sharing of scientific data underlying peer-reviewed publications and other findings?
YES / NO
Q2
Will the scientific data underlying peer-reviewed publications be shared by the time of publication, or by the end of the performance period?
YES / NO
Q3
Will shared scientific data be made available for at least as long as required by applicable data repository and/or journal policies?
YES / NO
Q4
If you answered "No" to Q1, Q2, or Q3 — or if sharing will be limited for any reason — describe the ethical, legal, or technical factors. Specify a particular reason. (300-word maximum)
EXPLAIN IF NO
Q5
If scientific data from human research participants will be shared — will privacy, rights, and confidentiality be protected (per NOT-OD-22-213), including use of access controls?
YES / NO / N/A
Q6
In a table: list key data types expected to be generated (including species and modality if known) and the repository where data may be managed and shared. (100-word maximum)
TABLE
Q7
For studies subject to the NIH Genomic Data Sharing (GDS) Policy: will you share large-scale human genomic data in a NIH-designated repository per accelerated GDS timelines?
YES / NO / N/A

FAIR Data Principles

The philosophical foundation of the NIH DMS Policy — NIH encourages practices consistent with FAIR

FAIR stands for Findable, Accessible, Interoperable, and Reusable. These principles, developed by Wilkinson et al. (2016), guide how data should be structured and shared to maximize their value to the research community.

F
Findable
  • Assign a persistent, unique identifier (DOI, accession number)
  • Use rich, searchable metadata
  • Register data in an indexed repository
A
Accessible
  • Data retrievable by identifier using standard protocols (HTTPS, SPARQL)
  • Metadata remain accessible even if data is restricted
  • Authentication and authorization clearly defined
I
Interoperable
  • Use community-accepted data formats and standards
  • Include controlled vocabularies or ontologies in metadata
  • Enable integration with other datasets
R
Reusable
  • Clear, accessible data usage license (CC0, CC-BY)
  • Detailed provenance and documentation for secondary use
  • Meets community standards for your research domain

✏️ Activity 1 FAIR Self-Check

Think about a recent dataset from your research (or a dataset type you expect to generate). For each FAIR principle, jot one concrete step you could take to make that data more FAIR before sharing.

Choosing a Data Repository

NIH strongly encourages use of established repositories — addressed directly in Q6 of the new format

NIH expects researchers to deposit data in established repositories that support long-term preservation and public (or controlled) access. The policy prefers domain-specific repositories when they exist.

Repository Type Examples Best For
NIH-Designated dbGaP, GEO, ImmPort, NCBI SRA, ClinicalTrials.gov Genomics, clinical trials, immunology, sequence data
Domain-Specific (non-NIH) ICPSR, OpenNeuro, UK Data Service, OSF Social science, neuroscience, survey and qualitative data
Generalist Repositories Zenodo, Figshare, Dryad Data types without a dedicated domain-specific home
NIH-Supported Institutional NIMH Data Archive, NDA, LONI Neuroimaging, developmental, and behavioral data
🔓

Free & Open Access

Repository must provide public access at no cost to end users

🆔

Persistent Identifier

Must assign DOIs or accession numbers for long-term citability

📦

Long-Term Preservation

Must support data availability for as long as repository/journal policies require

🔗 NIH Repository Finder: sharing.nih.gov → Selecting a Data Repository — Library staff can help you select the right repository for your data type.

Sharing Data from Human Research Participants

Extra considerations required in Q5 of the 2026 format — addressed in Element 5 of the original format

When your research involves human subjects, the DMSP must specifically describe how participant privacy, rights, and confidentiality will be protected throughout data management and sharing.

📋 Informed Consent Language

Consent forms should anticipate future data sharing. Include language about data being deposited in a public or controlled-access repository. IRBs and consent forms developed before 2023 may need revision for ongoing studies.

🔒 De-identification Requirements

Data shared publicly must be de-identified per HIPAA Safe Harbor or Expert Determination standards. NIH Certificates of Confidentiality can provide additional protections for sensitive research.

🗄️ Controlled Access Options

Sensitive data (genomic, clinical, mental health) may be deposited in controlled-access repositories such as dbGaP rather than open-access repositories, preserving both sharing and privacy.

📝 Document Justifiable Limitations

If full sharing is not possible due to consent or privacy constraints, document this clearly in Q4 (new format) or Element 5 (original format). A compelling rationale assessed by NIH is required.

Budgeting for Data Management & Sharing

Allowable costs can be requested across standard budget categories — no single DMS line item required as of Oct. 5, 2023

The DMS Policy explicitly allows NIH applicants to request funds for data management and sharing activities. Reasonable and allocable costs directly related to implementing the approved DMSP are allowable.

👩‍💻 Personnel

Staff time for data curation, documentation, quality control, de-identification, and repository deposit — including research coordinator or data manager roles

🔬 Consultant Services

Biostatistician, data manager, bioinformatician, or informatics specialist for data formatting and standards compliance

💻 Computer Services

Repository submission fees, cloud storage costs, secure data transfer, computing time for data processing before deposit

🖥️ Equipment

Hardware or storage systems needed specifically for data management activities described in the DMSP

📄 Formatting & Documentation

Converting data to community-accepted standards; creating data dictionaries, README files, codebooks, and metadata documentation

🏛️ Local Infrastructure

Institutional data management platforms with associated costs — such as REDCap or institutional cloud storage — when needed for secure local management

💡 Tip: Use the NIH DMS Cost Estimation Tool (available at grants.nih.gov) to build a justified budget narrative. Your DMS budget justification must align precisely with the activities described in your DMSP.

⚠️ Non-Compliance Consequences: May result in special terms and conditions added to the award, restrictions on future NIH funding, or other enforcement actions from NIH. Compliance is monitored through annual RPPRs and may be audited.

Sample DMSP Walk-Through (New 2026 Format)

Example: NIH R01 — health disparities study with survey and de-identified clinical data

Here is how a researcher would complete each question in the new structured format:

Q1 — Maximum sharing?
YES
Survey and de-identified clinical data will be deposited in ICPSR with open access following de-identification per HIPAA Safe Harbor standards.
Q2 — Shared by publication/end of period?
YES
Repository deposit planned upon manuscript acceptance. All datasets will be fully available no later than the project end date.
Q3 — Available long-term?
YES
ICPSR provides 25+ year preservation with DataCite DOIs per institutional repository policy.
Q4 — Any limitations?
N/A
No limitations on sharing are anticipated. All data will be de-identified before deposit.
Q5 — Human participant protections?
YES
Data de-identified per HIPAA Safe Harbor; consent forms include NIH-compliant data sharing language; no access controls needed for de-identified open data.
Q6 — Data types & repositories (table):
Expected Data TypeEstablished Repository
De-identified survey data (human participants)ICPSR — icpsr.umich.edu
De-identified clinical measurements (human participants)ICPSR or Zenodo (generalist)
Analysis code (R scripts)OSF or GitHub + Zenodo DOI

When Sharing Is Limited: Justifiable Factors

Q4 of the 2026 format — 300-word maximum for explaining limitations. A compelling rationale will be assessed by NIH.

NIH expects researchers to maximize data sharing, but acknowledges that certain factors may necessitate limiting sharing. Foreseeable limitations must be described proactively in the DMSP with a clear, specific rationale.

👥 Informed Consent

Consent does not permit future sharing, or limits the scope, type, or future research uses of the data. This includes existing consent for previously collected biospecimens.

🔒 Privacy & Safety

Sharing would compromise participant privacy or place them at unacceptable re-identification risk — even after de-identification. Certificates of Confidentiality may not be sufficient.

⚖️ Legal Restrictions

Federal, state, local, or Tribal law, regulation, or policy explicitly prohibits data disclosure. This includes FERPA for student records and certain state privacy laws.

📄 Third-Party Agreements

Existing agreements with co-funders, industry partners, repositories, or data providers restrict or prohibit sharing. Discuss with collaborators early to avoid unnecessary restrictions.

🧪 Data Quality

Data do not meet the quality standards sufficient to validate and replicate research findings, and sharing would create a risk of misinformation or misuse.

💾 Technical Barriers

File sizes, proprietary formats, or specialized infrastructure requirements make data sharing technically infeasible within the award period.

⚡ Important: "We don't want to share" or "the data are valuable" are not justifiable reasons. NIH staff will evaluate the rationale and may request revisions. State the specific reason with reference to the relevant law, consent language, or agreement.
