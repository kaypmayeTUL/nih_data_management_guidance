What Is the NIH DMS Policy?

Effective January 25, 2023 — a major departure from the 2003 policy

Jan 25, 2023Effective date
AllFunding levels covered
2 pagesMax plan length

The NIH Policy for Data Management and Sharing requires all NIH-funded or conducted research that generates scientific data to include a formal plan for how that data will be managed and shared throughout the project period and beyond.

⚡

Key change from 2003: The old policy only required data sharing plans for grants of $500,000 or more in direct costs per year. The 2023 policy applies to all grant applications that generate scientific data, regardless of funding level.

Data sharing accelerates discovery by enabling validation of results, reuse of hard-to-generate data, and exploration of new research questions. NIH aligns this policy with the FAIR data principles — Findable, Accessible, Interoperable, and Reusable.

📌

Individual NIH Institutes, Centers, and Offices (ICOs) may have additional requirements beyond the trans-NIH policy. Always review the specific Funding Opportunity Announcement (FOA) carefully.

Who Must Comply?

The policy applies broadly — but not universally

✔ Covered — Must Submit a DMSP

  • Research grants & competing renewals
  • Cooperative agreements (U mechanisms)
  • Some Career Development Awards (K awards)
  • Small Business SBIR/STTR grants
  • Other Transaction funding agreements (on/after Jan 25, 2023)
  • NIH Intramural Research Projects
  • Foreign institutions receiving NIH funds

✘ Not Covered — No DMSP Required

  • Training grants (T mechanisms)
  • Fellowship awards (F mechanisms)
  • Construction grants (C06)
  • Conference grants (R13)
  • Resource grants (G mechanisms)
  • Research-Related Infrastructure (S06)
  • Activities that do not generate scientific data
💡

When in doubt, check NIH Grants Policy Statement §8.2.3.1 or the NIH Data Sharing overview page. Library staff can help you confirm your activity code is covered.

Defining "Scientific Data"

The policy applies to scientific data — not everything your lab produces

Official NIH Definition

"The recorded factual material commonly accepted in the scientific community as of sufficient quality to validate and replicate research findings, regardless of whether the data are used to support scholarly publications."

✔ IS Scientific Data

  • Processed datasets used to support conclusions
  • Survey responses (when de-identified)
  • Genomic sequences, imaging data, clinical measurements
  • Code/software needed to reproduce findings
  • Metadata accompanying data files
  • Qualitative data (coded transcripts, interview data)

✘ NOT Scientific Data

  • Laboratory notebooks & preliminary analyses
  • Completed case report forms
  • Drafts of scientific papers
  • Plans for future research & peer reviews
  • Physical objects (specimens, slides, tissue)
  • Communications with colleagues
💡

Quick test: If you wouldn't share it to let another researcher replicate your findings, it's likely not scientific data under this definition.

Original DMSP Elements (NOT-OD-21-014)

Six narrative elements — applications due before May 25, 2026

📋

These six elements defined the original narrative-format DMSP. Applications with due dates before May 25, 2026 use this format. Applications due on or after May 25, 2026 use the new simplified Q1–Q7 format (next slide).

1

Data Type

Types, formats, volume, and preservation needs for data to be generated or used in the project.

2

Related Tools, Software & Code

Specialized tools or code needed to access or use the data; whether code itself will be shared.

3

Standards

Metadata standards, data dictionaries, unique identifiers, and community standards applied to data.

4

Data Preservation, Access & Timeline

Named repository, what will be shared, when it will be available, and how long it will be kept.

5

Access, Distribution & Reuse

Open vs. controlled access, licenses (CC0, CC-BY), and any limits on how others may reuse the data.

6

Oversight of DMS

Who at your institution (by role/title) will monitor compliance, with what frequency, and by what method.

2026 Format Update: NOT-OD-26-046

Effective for applications with due dates on or after May 25, 2026

⚡

What changed: NIH reviewed 1,100+ DMSPs and found many exceeded the 2-page limit with unnecessary narrative. The new format shifts to structured Yes/No questions — only requiring explanation when the answer is "No" or sharing will be limited. This supersedes NOT-OD-21-014.

Q1
Will there be maximum appropriate sharing of scientific data underlying publications and other findings?
YES / NO
Q2
Will data underlying publications be shared by time of publication (or end of performance period for other findings)?
YES / NO
Q3
Will shared data be available for at least as long as required by applicable repository and/or journal policies?
YES / NO
Q4
If any answer above is No, or if sharing will be limited — describe limitations (ethical, legal, technical). Max 300 words.
EXPLAIN IF NO
Q5
If human participant data will be shared — will privacy, rights, and confidentiality be protected per NOT-OD-22-213?
YES / NO / N/A
Q6
List key data types anticipated (species, modality if known) and repository where data may be shared. Table format, 100 words max.
TABLE
Q7
For studies subject to the NIH Genomic Data Sharing Policy — will you share large-scale human genomic data per GDS requirements?
YES / NO / N/A

FAIR Data Principles

The philosophical foundation of the NIH DMS Policy

NIH encourages data management and sharing practices to be consistent with FAIR principles. Being FAIR doesn't mean making everything open — it means ensuring data can be found, accessed under clear terms, combined with other data, and reused effectively.

FFindable
  • Assign a persistent, unique identifier (DOI, accession number)
  • Use rich, searchable metadata
  • Register data in an indexed repository
AAccessible
  • Data retrievable by identifier using open, standard protocols
  • Metadata remain accessible even if data is restricted
  • Authentication and authorization clearly defined
IInteroperable
  • Use community-accepted data standards & formats
  • Include controlled vocabularies or ontologies in metadata
  • Enable integration with other datasets
RReusable
  • Clear, accessible data usage license (e.g., CC0, CC-BY)
  • Detailed provenance & documentation for secondary use
  • Meets community standards for your research domain
💡

Being FAIR ≠ being open. Controlled-access data can still be FAIR as long as the metadata is findable and the access conditions are clearly specified. dbGaP data is a good example of FAIR controlled-access data.

Metadata Standards

Element 3 of the original DMSP — and essential for FAIR, findable, reusable data

Metadata is structured information that describes, contextualizes, and helps others find and use your data. Your DMSP must identify which metadata standards you will apply — and if no standard exists for your field, you must say so explicitly.

💡

Why it matters: Without consistent metadata, data deposited in a repository becomes nearly impossible for other researchers to find, interpret, or reuse. Good metadata is the backbone of FAIR data — it enables the F (Findable) and I (Interoperable) in FAIR.

Popular Metadata Standards by Discipline

Standard / SchemaDiscipline / Data TypeKey Features
Dublin Core General / cross-disciplinary; generalist repositories 15 simple elements (title, creator, date, subject, etc.); widely supported; good starting point for any field
DataCite Metadata Schema General / data repositories (Zenodo, Dryad, Figshare) Designed specifically for research data; supports DOI registration; includes funder, version, and license fields
DDI (Data Documentation Initiative) Social sciences, survey & quantitative data; ICPSR Richly describes variables, question wording, response categories, and methodology; XML-based; standard for ICPSR deposits
DCAT (Data Catalog Vocabulary) Government data, open data portals W3C standard; enables interoperability between data catalogs; RDF-based linked data
ISO 19115 / FGDC Geospatial / GIS data Describes spatial reference systems, geographic extent, resolution, and coordinate systems
MIAME / MINSEQE Genomics / microarray & sequencing data; GEO, SRA Minimum information standards for gene expression experiments; required by many journals and NIH repositories
DICOM Medical / clinical imaging data Industry standard for medical images (MRI, CT, PET); includes patient, acquisition, and equipment metadata
BIDS (Brain Imaging Data Structure) Neuroimaging; OpenNeuro, NDA Organizes MRI, fMRI, EEG data in a standardized file/folder structure with JSON sidecar metadata; required by OpenNeuro
EML (Ecological Metadata Language) Ecology / environmental science; EDI, DataONE Describes ecological datasets including sampling methods, taxonomic coverage, and geographic context
Darwin Core Biodiversity / species occurrence data; GBIF Community standard for sharing information about biological specimens; supports interoperability across biodiversity databases

📝 What to Include in Your DMSP

  • Name the specific standard(s) you will apply to each data type
  • Note whether the repository collects metadata automatically (e.g., ICPSR uses DDI; GEO uses MIAME)
  • Describe any controlled vocabularies, ontologies, or data dictionaries you will use
  • If no community standard exists, state: "No consensus metadata standard exists for this data type; project-specific documentation will be provided."

🔍 Metadata vs. Data Dictionaries

Metadata standards describe the dataset itself — who created it, when, under what conditions.

Data dictionaries / codebooks describe the variables within the dataset — what each column means, how it was measured, its units and permissible values.

Both are important in a DMSP. Many repositories require both for deposit.

💡

Tip for social scientists at Tulane: If you are depositing survey, interview, or quantitative data in ICPSR, your metadata will be encoded in DDI format automatically during the submission process. Zenodo and OSF use DataCite. Knowing your target repository simplifies your DMSP standards section considerably.

Choosing a Data Repository

NIH strongly encourages established repositories — required in Q6 of the new format

Repositories must support long-term preservation and public (or controlled) access. NIH prefers domain-specific repositories when available, followed by generalist repositories.

Repository TypeExamplesBest For
NIH-Designated SpecificdbGaP, GEO, ImmPort, NCBI SRA, ClinicalTrials.govGenomics, clinical, immunology, sequence data
Domain-Specific (non-NIH)ICPSR, OpenNeuro, UK Data Service, ANDSSocial science, neuroscience, survey data
Generalist RepositoriesZenodo, Figshare, Dryad, OSFData without a domain-specific home
NIH-Supported InstitutionalNIMH Data Archive (NDA), LONINeuroimaging, developmental, behavioral data

🔒 What Makes a Repository "Established"?

  • Provides free & open access (or controlled with clear process)
  • Issues unique persistent identifiers (DOIs)
  • Commits to long-term preservation
  • Enforces community metadata standards

Sharing Data from Human Research Participants

Extra considerations required in Q5 of the new format

When your research involves human subjects, the DMSP must address how participant privacy, rights, and confidentiality will be protected — even for data that will be shared.

📝 Informed Consent

Consent forms should anticipate future data sharing. Include language about data being deposited in a public or controlled-access repository. IRBs often have model language — check with the Tulane IRB.

🔒 De-identification

Data shared publicly must be de-identified under HIPAA Safe Harbor or Expert Determination standards. Certificates of Confidentiality (CoCs) may provide additional protections for sensitive data.

🔐 Controlled Access

Sensitive data (genomic, clinical) may be shared through controlled-access repositories such as dbGaP, where users must apply for access. This satisfies sharing requirements without exposing identifiable data.

⚖️ Justifiable Limitations

Existing consent language, re-identification risk, legal restrictions, or Tribal data sovereignty concerns can justify limiting sharing. Document these in Q4 with a compelling rationale.

⚠️

Submitting your study to ClinicalTrials.gov does not satisfy NIH DMS policy requirements. These are separate and distinct obligations.

Budgeting for Data Management & Sharing

Reasonable DMS costs can be requested in standard NIH budget categories

The DMS Policy explicitly allows NIH applicants to request funds for data management and sharing activities. As of October 5, 2023, no single DMS line item is required — costs can be distributed across standard budget categories.

👤 Personnel

Staff time for data curation, documentation, and de-identification. Include % effort in budget justification.

🔬 Consultant Services

Biostatistician, data manager, or informatics specialist supporting DMS activities.

💻 Computer Services

Repository submission fees, cloud storage costs, or computing time for data processing and formatting.

🖥️ Equipment

Hardware or storage needed specifically for data management (not general lab computing).

📄 Formatting & Documentation

Converting data to community-standard formats; writing data dictionaries, READMEs, codebooks.

🏛️ Local Infrastructure

Institutional data management platforms or specialized storage with associated recurring costs.

🧮

Use NIH's DMS Cost Estimation Tool at nih.gov to help build your budget justification. Your budget narrative must align with what you describe in your DMSP.

Annual RPPRs must include DMS compliance reporting as of October 1, 2024

Revising your plan: If DMS activities change significantly (switching repositories, new limitations), submit a formal revision via the Prior Approval Module in eRA Commons at least 30 days before the change. See NOT-OD-24-176.

Non-compliance consequences: Special award conditions, restrictions on future funding decisions, or other enforcement actions from NIH.

When Sharing Is Limited: Justifiable Factors

Q4 of the new format — 300-word limit for explaining limitations

NIH expects researchers to maximize data sharing, but acknowledges that certain factors may necessitate limiting sharing. Foreseeable limitations must be documented with a compelling rationale.

📝 Informed Consent

Existing or anticipated consent does not permit sharing, or limits the scope of sharing and future research use. May apply to previously collected biospecimens.

🔒 Privacy & Safety

Sharing would compromise participant privacy or create re-identification risk that de-identification or Certificates of Confidentiality cannot adequately mitigate.

⚖️ Legal Restrictions

Explicit federal, state, local, or Tribal law, regulation, or policy prohibits data disclosure. Includes Tribal data sovereignty considerations.

📑 Existing Agreements

Agreements with third-party funders, partners, or repositories restrict sharing — such as industry co-funding or licensing limitations attached to research materials.

🔬 Data Quality

Data do not meet quality or completeness standards sufficient to validate and replicate research findings — sharing would be misleading or unhelpful to the scientific community.

💻 Technical Barriers

File sizes, formats, or infrastructure constraints make sharing technically infeasible. Must be genuinely prohibitive, not merely inconvenient.

💡

SBIR/STTR recipients may withhold applicable data for 20 years after the award date per the SBIR/STTR Program Policy Directive (May 2019).
